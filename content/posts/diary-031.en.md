Today, I have finally graduated in the truest sense of the word!

No need to lament the rapid passage of time, nor to regret past failures. Here, please allow me to briefly tell the story of a youth who entered high school with impure motives but was ultimately transformed by grace (the following is the draft of the graduation speech, with minor edits).

To put it straight, most of my reasons for leaving public education were due to the excessive pressure, rather than some noble cause of pioneering Christian education in China. There were countless moments when I would wonder how I would be doing today if I had stayed in the public system and taken that broad avenue. Modestly speaking, my learning ability is quite strong, so the academic achievements I could have attained in public school might have been more outstanding than they are today. Once, while I was thinking about this, Teacher Zhang, who was sitting nearby, said, "Modern education can indeed produce a top scorer, but it might be a top scorer who commits suicide." Ah, humans are just like this, always infinitely romanticizing the things they cannot have, while forgetting to be grateful for and cherish what they already possess.

What have I received, then? The changes over these three years are so numerous, and even being able to stand here today was once something I couldn't have imagined. If I had stayed in the public system and survived, I would now in all probability have become one of those who say, "If I were still a high schooler, I would say it's simple, but now that I'm a college student..." We often joke that high school is the peak of our life's knowledge, but most of the time, once the Gaokao is over, everything vanishes into thin air.

Though I do not know what the future holds, I am certain that everything I learned during these three years at Gaojia will absolutely not vanish like a passing cloud. Because what I have acquired is not merely knowledge, but more importantly, how to conduct myself, how to be independent, how to face difficulties, and most crucially of all, how to commune with Him.

At our current age, too many of our peers are gradually becoming friends with the world. This is indeed a long-drawn-out spiritual warfare, but just as old John reminds us in his epistle, the world and its desires will eventually pass away, but the one who does the will of God lives forever.

I understand that I was, am, and will continue to be in this battle. However, what accompanies this is not anxiety and restlessness, but rather faith and faithfulness. I do not regret choosing this path, even if it originally stemmed from sinful motives, for "all things work together for good." I believe there are beautiful preparations ahead, and He will surely guide the path before me, and before all of us.
