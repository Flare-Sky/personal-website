Growth is a process of constantly leaving regrets behind.

Recently, worries seem to be piling up: staying up too late yesterday led to failing to wake up early, being late for gatherings and dozing off; using my phone too much normally led to overdue data charges, coincidentally my phone was suspended and couldn't be recharged; the first half of my flight back to Chengde was canceled, and upon communication, I realized I hadn't bought a direct flight and had to pay a cancellation fee; rushing onto the bus at the stop only to find it was going the wrong way, and getting off to discover my bus card was overdue again; rushing to Yuhang District only to learn that rehearsal was today, resulting in having to temporarily switch roles and fill in; teammates had already figured out the longitude and latitude for a problem, but due to inaccurate Google Map positioning, successfully led a large group of people on a wild goose chase; missing the last bus after getting off the subway, and riding a shared bike home only to find all docking stations were full, forcing a detour... No one probably wants to hear me complain, but listing them out, I realize I've been quite unlucky (

Walking along the river on the way home, calming down to examine all of this, I felt the weight of the word "growth." I always want to kill two birds with one stone, yet I am also somewhat forgetful, often achieving half the results with twice the effort while failing to meet others' expectations; these facts haven't taught me humility, but instead fueled my vanity, my desire for others' recognition and praise. Can such a person as myself truly respond to others' expectations and help them?

Yesterday, I completed the playthrough of "Finding Paradise." Although it's not as famous as its predecessor, "To the Moon," I felt a deeper resonance and shed tears during it. I won't spoil it here, and I hope friends who read this short essay can truly experience the protagonist's life full of regrets and disappointments. I don't have a Faye by my side, but I am surrounded by a group of people who love me, and there are also eyes and sighs from heaven.

Suddenly, I remembered saying to someone, "No matter how busy you are, don't forget to look up at the stars." Looking up at the sky, today's Hangzhou sky was exceptionally clear in the chilly air. I could vaguely make out the fierce eyes of Sirius and the heroic posture of Orion. When the moonlight dims, the starlight becomes clearer; perhaps, we don't all need to shine constantly.

After all, it's those regrets that have made me who I am today.
